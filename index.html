Retail Vacancy is Up Around Every Corner, Astoria Representatives Blame Rising Costs and Predatory Behavior

by Ashley Rodriguez

A recent report by the New York City Comptroller shows a 10 year high in retail vacancy citywide, with the highest rates of vacany outside of Manhattan.



In a September report released by the New York City's Comptroller Scott Stringer, it was revealed that there are currently 27,560 square feet of vacant storefronts in Astoria, a nine-year high.

The culprit, according to District 22 Councilman, Costa Constantinides, is predatory developers who have, as Stringer report demonstrates, driven up retail rent by 39% over the last year from $29.91 a square foot on average to $41.66. The report also notes that property tax on retail space in the area has also climbed, going up by 69% between 2017-2018, creating a greater burden for mom and pop shops.

"Change is the one constant in New York City - and sometimes, change can be overwhelming,"" said Stringer in a statement. "Even as our economy has grown, many mom-and-pop stores have been left behind, transforming spaces once owned by local small businesses into barren storefronts.""





Both Stringer and Constantinides agree that what's most important about this report is that it can help the city move in a direction that supports small businesses, as retail trends continue to change, in New York City and for Constantinides, in his Queens neighborhood of Astoria.

A fear echoed among residents like Heather Job, 26, is of big box stores moving into Astoria and further exacerbating retail vacancy in the area, specifically, a Target invasion. Target is currently hoping to build a 47,000 square feet store on 31st in the Ditmars area of Astoria.

"I don't want a Target in my neighborhood," said Job, who chose Astoria when moving to the city from Colorado due to its small-town charm. "I don't want it raising my rent."
